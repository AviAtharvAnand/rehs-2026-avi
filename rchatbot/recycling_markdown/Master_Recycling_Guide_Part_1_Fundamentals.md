# Master Recycling Guide

## Part 1 --- Recycling Fundamentals

### Purpose

This guide serves as a general recycling reference for the United
States. It explains how recycling works, how to prepare materials
correctly, and why local recycling rules sometimes differ. City-specific
guides should always take precedence when they conflict with this
document.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# What Is Recycling?

Recycling is the process of collecting used materials, sorting them,
processing them into raw materials, and manufacturing them into new
products. Recycling helps conserve natural resources, reduce landfill
use, lower greenhouse gas emissions, and save energy.

Many everyday products---including aluminum cans, cardboard boxes, glass
bottles, and office paper---can be made into new products multiple times
when properly recycled.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# The Recycling Process

1.  Residents place accepted materials into a recycling container.
2.  Materials are transported to a Materials Recovery Facility (MRF).
3.  Machines and workers sort paper, cardboard, plastics, metals, and
    glass.
4.  Contaminants are removed.
5.  Materials are baled and shipped to manufacturers.
6.  Manufacturers produce new goods from recycled material.

Contamination at any stage can reduce the quality of recovered materials
and may cause otherwise recyclable items to be discarded.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Why Recycling Matters

Benefits include:

-   Conserves trees, metals, petroleum, and minerals.
-   Uses less energy than producing many materials from raw resources.
-   Reduces landfill space.
-   Helps reduce greenhouse gas emissions.
-   Supports a circular economy by keeping valuable materials in use.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# The Five Principles

## 1. Empty

Remove food and liquids before recycling.

Examples: - Empty beverage bottles - Empty yogurt containers - Empty
soup cans

## 2. Clean

Containers do not need to be spotless but should be free of large
amounts of food residue.

Examples: - Rinse pasta sauce jars. - Scrape peanut butter jars. - Empty
detergent bottles.

## 3. Dry

Paper and cardboard should remain dry because wet fibers are difficult
to recycle.

## 4. Loose

Place recyclables directly into the recycling cart unless your local
program specifically requests otherwise.

Avoid placing recyclable items inside plastic bags.

## 5. Know Local Rules

Recycling programs differ.

Examples: - One city may accept plastic tubs while another does not. -
One city may recycle cartons while another may not. - Glass collection
varies by community.

Always follow your local government's recycling guidance.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# What Is Contamination?

Contamination occurs when non-recyclable materials are mixed with
recyclable materials.

Common contaminants include:

-   Food waste
-   Plastic bags
-   Garden hoses
-   Clothing
-   Batteries
-   Electronics
-   Liquids
-   Medical waste

Contamination increases sorting costs and can result in recyclable loads
being rejected.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Wish-Cycling

Wish-cycling means placing an item into recycling because you hope it is
recyclable.

Examples include:

-   Plastic toys
-   Garden hoses
-   Coffee pods
-   Disposable cutlery
-   Broken dishes

When uncertain:

1.  Check your local recycling website.
2.  Contact your waste hauler.
3.  Use a verified recycling search tool.
4.  If still uncertain, do not place the item in curbside recycling.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# General Preparation Checklist

Before recycling an item:

-   Empty contents.
-   Remove excess food.
-   Rinse if needed.
-   Keep materials dry.
-   Flatten cardboard.
-   Keep recyclables loose.
-   Never place hazardous waste in recycling.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Common Questions

**Can dirty recyclables be recycled?**

Sometimes lightly soiled containers can be recycled after rinsing, but
excessive food contamination may cause rejection.

**Do labels need to be removed?**

Usually no. Labels are removed during processing.

**Should I crush cans?**

Many programs accept lightly crushed cans, but follow local guidance.

**Can I bag my recyclables?**

Usually no. Plastic bags interfere with sorting equipment.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Key Terms

**MRF (Materials Recovery Facility):** A facility that sorts recyclable
materials.

**Contamination:** Non-recyclable material mixed with recyclables.

**Circular Economy:** A system that keeps materials in use through reuse
and recycling.

**Wish-cycling:** Placing questionable items into recycling in the hope
they are recyclable.
