# Master Recycling Guide

## Part 5 --- Organics & Composting

### Overview

Organics recycling diverts food scraps and yard waste from landfills and
turns them into compost or renewable energy. Many communities now
provide a separate organics or green bin program. Requirements vary by
location, but composting is becoming increasingly common across the
United States.

> **Important:** Only place materials accepted by your local organics
> program into the compost bin.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# What Are Organics?

Organic materials are biodegradable items that naturally decompose.

Common examples include:

-   Fruits
-   Vegetables
-   Bread
-   Rice
-   Pasta
-   Coffee grounds
-   Tea bags
-   Eggshells
-   Meat
-   Fish
-   Poultry
-   Dairy products
-   Yard trimmings
-   Leaves
-   Grass
-   Small branches
-   Flowers

------------------------------------------------------------------------

Many municipal compost programs accept:

-   Apple cores
-   Banana peels
-   Citrus peels
-   Vegetable scraps
-   Salad
-   Bread
-   Tortillas
-   Rice
-   Pasta
-   Meat scraps
-   Bones
-   Seafood shells
-   Coffee grounds
-   Coffee filters
-   Tea bags (without plastic)
-   Eggshells

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Yard Waste

Often accepted:

-   Grass clippings
-   Leaves
-   Weeds
-   Flowers
-   Plants
-   Small branches
-   Shrubs
-   Untreated wood chips

Usually not accepted:

-   Painted wood
-   Pressure-treated wood
-   Large tree stumps
-   Soil (varies)

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Food-Soiled Paper

Many compost programs accept:

-   Paper napkins
-   Paper towels
-   Greasy pizza box portions
-   Uncoated paper plates
-   Compostable paper food containers

Do not include plastic-coated or wax-coated paper unless your local
program specifically accepts it.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Compostable Products

Certified compostable products may include:

-   Compostable food-service ware
-   Compostable bags (only where accepted)
-   Compostable plates and bowls

Items labeled "biodegradable" are **not automatically compostable** in
municipal programs.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Usually NOT Accepted

Keep these out of the compost bin unless your local program explicitly
allows them:

-   Plastic bags
-   Glass
-   Metal
-   Electronics
-   Batteries
-   Pet waste (varies)
-   Diapers
-   Vacuum dust
-   Treated wood
-   Plastic utensils

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Home Composting

Home compost systems commonly accept:

-   Fruit and vegetable scraps
-   Coffee grounds
-   Tea leaves
-   Dry leaves
-   Grass
-   Small garden plants

Avoid adding:

-   Oils
-   Large quantities of meat
-   Diseased plants
-   Pet waste

A healthy compost pile should contain a balance of "greens"
(nitrogen-rich materials) and "browns" (carbon-rich materials).

# Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I compost coffee grounds?

Usually yes.

### Can I compost eggshells?

Usually yes.

### Can I compost meat?

Many municipal composting programs accept meat, but many home compost
piles do not.

### Can I compost pizza boxes?

Greasy portions are often compostable.

### Can I compost paper towels?

Often yes if they were used with food or water and are free of harsh
chemicals.

### Can I compost tea bags?

Only if they do not contain plastic mesh.

### Can I compost compostable plastic forks?

Only if accepted by your local commercial composting facility.

# Common Mistakes

Avoid placing these in organics bins:

-   Plastic produce stickers
-   Plastic bags
-   Glass containers
-   Metal cans
-   Batteries
-   Electronics
-   Garden hoses
-   Treated lumber

# Best Practices

-   Remove packaging before composting food.
-   Empty food scraps directly into the organics bin.
-   Keep plastic contamination out.
-   Follow local organics program rules.
-   Use home composting where appropriate.
