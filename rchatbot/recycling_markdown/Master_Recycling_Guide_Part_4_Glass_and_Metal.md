# Master Recycling Guide

## Part 4 --- Glass & Metal

### Overview

Glass and metal are among the most valuable recyclable materials because
they can often be recycled repeatedly without significant loss of
quality. Proper preparation and understanding which items are accepted
helps improve recovery rates.

> **Important:** Local programs differ. Follow your local recycling
> guidelines if they conflict with this guide.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Glass

## Commonly Accepted

Most curbside recycling programs accept:

-   Glass beverage bottles
-   Wine bottles
-   Beer bottles
-   Glass food jars
-   Salsa jars
-   Pickle jars
-   Jelly jars
-   Pasta sauce jars

Preparation:

-   Empty contents.
-   Rinse food residue.
-   Remove excessive food.
-   Metal lids may often be recycled separately.

## Usually NOT Accepted

These items are often made from different types of glass and should not
go into curbside recycling:

-   Drinking glasses
-   Wine glasses
-   Ceramics
-   Porcelain
-   Mirrors
-   Window glass
-   Pyrex bakeware
-   Crystal
-   Light bulbs
-   Fluorescent tubes

## Broken Glass

Broken bottles and jars are accepted in some communities but not others.
Broken windows, mirrors, and ceramics usually require disposal as trash
or through special recycling programs.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Metal

## Commonly Accepted

-   Aluminum beverage cans
-   Steel food cans
-   Tin cans
-   Clean aluminum foil
-   Clean foil trays
-   Empty aerosol cans (where accepted)
-   Metal lids

Preparation:

-   Empty containers.
-   Rinse food residue.
-   Crumple small pieces of foil into a ball to improve sorting if
    recommended locally.

## Usually NOT Accepted

-   Paint cans containing paint
-   Propane cylinders
-   Helium tanks
-   Fire extinguishers
-   Scrap metal that exceeds local size limits
-   Pressurized containers that are not empty

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Aluminum

Aluminum is one of the most recyclable materials.

Examples:

-   Soda cans
-   Sparkling water cans
-   Pet food cans (aluminum)
-   Foil
-   Baking trays

Recycling aluminum saves significant energy compared to producing new
aluminum from raw ore.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Steel

Common examples:

-   Soup cans
-   Vegetable cans
-   Coffee cans
-   Pet food cans
-   Cookie tins

Remove food residue before recycling.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Aerosol Cans

Many programs accept empty aerosol cans.

Accepted examples:

-   Cooking spray
-   Hair spray
-   Shaving cream

Do not recycle partially full or hazardous aerosol containers unless
specifically instructed.

# Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I recycle a glass bottle?

Usually yes.

### Can I recycle a glass jar?

Usually yes.

### Can I recycle a mirror?

Usually no.

### Can I recycle a ceramic mug?

Usually no.

### Can I recycle a drinking glass?

Usually no.

### Can I recycle aluminum foil?

Usually yes if clean.

### Can I recycle a foil tray?

Usually yes if clean.

### Can I recycle a soup can?

Yes after emptying and rinsing.

### Can I recycle a paint can?

Only if completely empty and accepted locally; cans containing paint
require hazardous waste disposal.

### Can I recycle a propane cylinder?

No. Use approved collection programs.

# Common Mistakes

Avoid placing these in curbside recycling:

-   Window panes
-   Mirrors
-   Ceramics
-   Pyrex
-   Crystal
-   Full aerosol cans
-   Gas cylinders
-   Metal tools (unless locally accepted)

# Best Practices

-   Empty all containers.
-   Rinse food residue.
-   Separate hazardous metal containers.
-   Keep glass free from non-glass materials.
-   Follow local guidance for broken glass.
