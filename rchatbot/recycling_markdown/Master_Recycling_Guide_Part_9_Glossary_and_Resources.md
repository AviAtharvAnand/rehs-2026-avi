# Master Recycling Guide

## Part 9 --- Recycling Symbols, Glossary & Resources

### Overview

This final section summarizes common recycling symbols, important
terminology, preparation checklists, decision-making guidance, and
trusted resources.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Recycling Symbols

## Mobius Loop (♻)

The universal recycling symbol indicates that a product **may** be
recyclable or contains recycled material. It does **not** guarantee
curbside acceptance.

## Plastic Resin Codes

  Code   Plastic Type   Common Examples   Typical Acceptance
  ------ -------------- ----------------- -----------------------------------
  #1     PET            Water bottles     Widely accepted
  #2     HDPE           Milk jugs         Widely accepted
  #3     PVC            Vinyl pipe        Rarely accepted
  #4     LDPE           Plastic bags      Store drop-off only in many areas
  #5     PP             Yogurt tubs       Increasingly accepted
  #6     PS             Foam cups         Usually not accepted
  #7     Other          Mixed plastics    Varies

Remember: the number identifies the resin, **not** whether it belongs in
your curbside recycling.

# Common Recycling Terms

**Circular Economy** -- Keeping materials in use through reuse, repair,
remanufacturing, and recycling.

**Contamination** -- Non-recyclable material mixed with recyclables.

**Compost** -- Organic material broken down into nutrient-rich soil
amendment.

**Curbside Recycling** -- Collection of recyclables from homes or
businesses.

**E-waste** -- Electronic devices requiring specialized recycling.

**Household Hazardous Waste (HHW)** -- Household products that are
toxic, flammable, corrosive, or reactive.

**Materials Recovery Facility (MRF)** -- Facility where recyclables are
sorted before being sold to manufacturers.

**Organics** -- Food scraps and yard waste collected for composting.

**Wish-cycling** -- Placing questionable items into recycling hoping
they are recyclable.

# Quick Preparation Checklist

Before recycling:

-   Empty containers.
-   Rinse away food residue.
-   Keep paper and cardboard dry.
-   Flatten cardboard boxes.
-   Keep recyclables loose unless instructed otherwise.
-   Remove hazardous materials.
-   Follow local recycling rules.

# Decision Guide

## Recycle

Examples:

-   Paper
-   Cardboard
-   Glass bottles
-   Metal cans
-   Plastic bottles and containers accepted locally

## Compost

Examples:

-   Food scraps
-   Coffee grounds
-   Leaves
-   Grass
-   Food-soiled paper (where accepted)

## Trash

Examples:

-   Diapers
-   Pet waste
-   Plastic film not accepted locally
-   Multi-layer snack wrappers
-   Broken ceramics

## Special Collection

Examples:

-   Batteries
-   Electronics
-   Paint
-   Motor oil
-   Propane cylinders
-   Tires
-   Mattresses

# Common Causes of Contamination

-   Food left in containers
-   Plastic bags in recycling carts
-   Garden hoses
-   Clothing
-   Electronics
-   Liquids
-   Batteries

Reducing contamination helps improve recycling efficiency and worker
safety.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Tips for Better Recycling

-   Buy reusable products.
-   Choose products with less packaging.
-   Repair items before replacing them.
-   Donate usable goods.
-   Buy products made with recycled content.
-   Avoid single-use plastics when practical.

# Using This Guide

This master guide provides general recycling best practices for the
United States.

Because recycling programs differ between communities, always:

1.  Check your city or county recycling website.
2.  Follow your local waste hauler's instructions.
3.  Use local guidance whenever it conflicts with general
    recommendations.

Following local rules helps reduce contamination and ensures recyclable
materials are processed correctly.
